RICE & ICE Scoring

Prioritising systematically when resources are tight

RICE (Reach × Impact × Confidence / Effort) and ICE (Impact × Confidence × Ease) are scoring frameworks that replace gut-feel prioritisation with structured thinking. RICE is thorough; ICE is fast. In a resource-constrained marketplace like Zomato, choosing the right framework for the right decision matters.

How to apply it

Use RICE for major roadmap decisions where you need buy-in. Use ICE for rapid prioritisation of growth experiments. In both cases: be brutally honest about Confidence — overconfidence is the most common scoring mistake. When in doubt, score lower.

Example

Zomato considers adding a grocery delivery feature. RICE: High reach (millions of users), medium impact (new behaviour), low confidence (unproven demand), high effort (new logistics). The score says: test small before going big.

RICE vs ICE Decision Guide

  1. Use RICE when — You have real user data for Reach. Enterprise, mature products.
  2. Use ICE when — Moving fast, early-stage, gut-informed decisions. Speed over precision.
  3. Reach — (RICE only) Users impacted per quarter. Pull from analytics, not guesses.
  4. Impact — Effect on target metric per user. Score consistently across all items.
  5. Confidence — Evidence quality. 100%=A/B tested, 80%=user research, 50%=intuition.
  6. Effort / Ease — RICE divides by effort (higher=harder). ICE uses ease (higher=easier).
  7. Calibration Session — Score all items together. Relative ranking matters more than absolute scores.

MoSCoW Method

Scoping what matters in a fast-moving market

MoSCoW (Must, Should, Could, Won't) forces you to draw a hard line between what's essential for launch and what's a luxury. In food-tech, where speed-to-market can make or break a feature, MoSCoW prevents scope creep and keeps teams focused on what moves the needle.

How to apply it

List every requirement stakeholders want. Force the conversation: "If we launch without this, does the product fail?" Must = yes. Should = it hurts but works. Could = nice later. Won't = explicitly parked. A good MoSCoW has far fewer Musts than people expect.

Example

Zomato launches a subscription service. Must Have: discounted delivery, billing system. Should Have: restaurant-specific perks. Could Have: priority customer support. Won't Have: exclusive menu items (too complex to coordinate).

MoSCoW Sprint Planning

  1. Must Have — Sprint fails without these. Core functionality. Non-negotiable.
  2. Should Have — High value but sprint still viable without. Build if time allows.
  3. Could Have — Nice additions. First to cut when estimates slip. Backlog for next sprint.
  4. Won't Have — Explicitly excluded. Prevents scope creep mid-sprint. Document for later.
  5. Effort Budget — Must = max 60% of sprint capacity. Leaves room for unknowns.
  6. Daily Re-check — If a Must slips, demote a Should. Never add scope without removing scope.

Kano Model

What delights users vs. what they just expect

The Kano Model reveals which features create disproportionate satisfaction (Delighters), which scale linearly with investment (Performance), and which are simply expected (Basics). In a two-sided marketplace, you need to apply Kano separately for each side — what delights restaurants may not delight customers.

How to apply it

For each feature, ask both sides: "How would you feel if this existed?" and "How would you feel if it didn't?" A feature that's a Delighter for customers but a Basic for restaurants needs different positioning for each audience.

Example

Live order tracking was once a Delighter for Zomato users — "wow, I can see my rider!" Now it's a Basic — users are frustrated if it's missing. Meanwhile, for restaurants, analytics dashboards are still a Delighter most don't expect.

Kano Survey Method

  1. Functional Question — "If [feature] existed, how would you feel?" (Like / Expect / Neutral / Tolerate / Dislike)
  2. Dysfunctional Question — "If [feature] did NOT exist, how would you feel?" Same scale.
  3. Classification Table — Cross-reference answers to classify: Must-Be, Performance, Attractive, Indifferent, Reverse.
  4. Prioritise by Type — Must-Be first (table stakes). Then Performance (differentiators). Then Attractive (delighters).
  5. Decay Warning — Today's Attractive becomes tomorrow's Must-Be. Features drift toward expectation over time.
  6. Sample Size — 20-30 respondents per segment is usually sufficient for clear patterns.

Marketplace Strategy

Balancing two sides of a platform simultaneously

Marketplace strategy is about managing the chicken-and-egg problem: you need supply (restaurants) to attract demand (customers), and vice versa. Key decisions involve subsidies, take rates, exclusivity, and deciding which side to prioritise at each growth stage.

How to apply it

Ask: Which side of the marketplace is harder to acquire? That's your constrained side — invest there first. What's the right take rate that keeps both sides happy? Are we solving for growth (subsidise) or profitability (optimise)? Can we create switching costs without locking users in unfairly?

Example

Zomato must decide: lower commission rates to attract more restaurants (improving supply), or offer deeper discounts to attract more customers (improving demand)? The answer depends on which side is the bottleneck in each city.

Marketplace Flywheel Template

  1. Supply Acquisition — How do you attract sellers/providers? Incentives, onboarding, tools?
  2. Demand Generation — How do you attract buyers? Marketing, referrals, organic?
  3. Matching Quality — How well does the platform connect supply and demand? Relevance, speed?
  4. Transaction Experience — Payment, delivery, dispute resolution. Friction kills marketplaces.
  5. Chicken-and-Egg — Which side to subsidise first? Start with the harder side to acquire.
  6. Flywheel Trigger — At what point does growth become self-reinforcing? Define the metric.
  7. Take Rate — Commission percentage. Too high = disintermediation. Too low = unsustainable.
